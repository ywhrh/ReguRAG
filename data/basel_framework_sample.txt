Basel III Capital Requirements — Key Provisions (Sample)

1. Minimum Capital Requirements

Under the Basel III framework, banks must maintain the following minimum capital ratios
at all times:

- Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) capital ratio: minimum 4.5% of risk-weighted assets (RWA).
- Tier 1 capital ratio: minimum 6.0% of RWA.
- Total capital ratio: minimum 8.0% of RWA.

These minimums must be maintained on a solo basis and on a consolidated group basis.

2. Capital Buffers

In addition to the minimum requirements, Basel III introduced the following capital buffers,
all of which must be met with CET1 capital:

(a) Capital Conservation Buffer (CCB): 2.5% of RWA. Banks that fail to maintain this buffer
    face automatic restrictions on earnings distributions (dividends, share buybacks,
    and discretionary bonus payments). The restrictions are graduated — the closer a bank
    is to the minimum requirement, the more severe the distribution constraints.

(b) Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB): ranges from 0% to 2.5% of RWA, set by national
    authorities. It is activated during periods of excessive credit growth and released
    during downturns to support lending. When a bank is subject to a positive CCyB,
    the buffer must be met with CET1 capital; failure to maintain it triggers the same
    distribution restrictions as the conservation buffer.

(c) Global Systemically Important Bank (G-SIB) Surcharge: ranges from 1% to 3.5% of RWA,
    determined annually by the Financial Stability Board (FSB) based on a five-category
    assessment methodology (size, interconnectedness, substitutability, complexity,
    and cross-jurisdictional activity). The surcharge must be met with CET1 capital.

3. Capital Quality — Definition of CET1

Common Equity Tier 1 capital is defined as:
- Paid-up ordinary shares (common stock) issued by the bank.
- Stock surplus (share premium) resulting from the issue of CET1 instruments.
- Retained earnings.
- Accumulated other comprehensive income (AOCI) and other disclosed reserves.
- Common shares issued by consolidated subsidiaries and held by third parties
  (minority interest), subject to specific recognition limits.
- Regulatory adjustments (deductions), including goodwill, other intangible assets,
  deferred tax assets, and certain investments in the capital of financial entities.

4. Leverage Ratio

Basel III introduced a non-risk-based leverage ratio as a supplementary measure to the
risk-based capital framework. The minimum Tier 1 leverage ratio is 3% of total exposures.
G-SIBs are subject to an additional leverage ratio buffer equal to 50% of their G-SIB
risk-weighted surcharge (e.g., a bank with a 2% G-SIB surcharge faces a 1% leverage
ratio buffer, implying a minimum leverage ratio of 4%).

The exposure measure includes both on-balance-sheet exposures and off-balance-sheet items
(converted using specified credit conversion factors) and derivatives (measured using
the Standardised Approach for Counterparty Credit Risk, SA-CCR).

5. Liquidity Standards

(a) Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR): requires banks to hold sufficient high-quality liquid
    assets (HQLA) to cover net cash outflows over a 30-day stress period. The minimum
    LCR is 100%. HQLA is divided into Level 1 assets (e.g., cash, central bank reserves,
    highest-rated sovereign debt — no haircut) and Level 2 assets (subject to minimum
    haircuts of 15% for Level 2A and 25% or more for Level 2B). Level 2 assets may
    not exceed 40% of the total HQLA stock; Level 2B assets may not exceed 15%.

(b) Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR): requires banks to maintain a stable funding profile
    relative to their on- and off-balance-sheet activities. Available Stable Funding (ASF)
    must equal or exceed Required Stable Funding (RSF), i.e., NSFR ≥ 100%.
    The NSFR is designed to reduce funding risk over a one-year horizon.

6. Large Exposures Framework

Under the Basel large exposures framework:
- A bank's exposure to a single counterparty must not exceed 25% of its Tier 1 capital.
- Exposures between G-SIBs (to another G-SIB) must not exceed 15% of Tier 1 capital.
- Exposures to a connected group of counterparties are aggregated and subject to the same
  25% limit applied to the consolidated group.

Banks must identify and aggregate all exposures to the same counterparty regardless of
the legal form, including loans, bonds, derivatives, off-balance-sheet commitments,
and eligible netting sets.

7. Disclosure Requirements (Pillar 3)

Banks are required to make public disclosures covering:
- Capital adequacy, including the breakdown of regulatory capital and RWA by risk type.
- Risk profile and risk management framework for each material risk category.
- Remuneration policies and practices.
- Asset encumbrance.

Disclosures must be published at least annually, with certain quantitative disclosures
on a semi-annual or quarterly basis. G-SIBs and other large internationally active banks
face enhanced frequency requirements. All disclosures must be published in an accessible
location (typically the bank's investor relations website) and must be consistent with
information used internally by management.

Basel III — Operational Risk Capital Requirements (Sample)

1. Standardised Approach (SA) for Operational Risk

From January 1, 2023, banks must use the Standardised Approach (SA) to calculate
operational risk capital requirements. The SA replaces the Basic Indicator Approach,
Standardised Approach (old), and Advanced Measurement Approach (AMA).

The SA capital requirement is calculated as:

  Operational Risk Capital = Business Indicator Component (BIC) × Internal Loss Multiplier (ILM)

(a) Business Indicator Component (BIC):
    The Business Indicator (BI) is based on three components:
    - Interest, Lease and Dividend Component (ILDC)
    - Services Component (SC)
    - Financial Component (FC)

    The BIC is derived by applying marginal coefficients to the BI in three buckets:
    - BI ≤ €1 billion: coefficient of 12%
    - €1 billion < BI ≤ €30 billion: coefficient of 15%
    - BI > €30 billion: coefficient of 18%

(b) Internal Loss Multiplier (ILM):
    The ILM adjusts the BIC based on the bank's historical loss experience.
    It is calculated as:
      ILM = ln(exp(1) − 1 + (Loss Component / BIC)^0.8)
    where the Loss Component equals 15× the average annual operational risk losses
    over the past 10 years.

    National supervisors may choose to set ILM = 1 for all banks in their jurisdiction,
    thereby making the capital requirement equal to the BIC alone.

2. Definition of Operational Risk Losses

For the purpose of the SA, operational risk losses are defined as losses resulting from
inadequate or failed internal processes, people, and systems, or from external events.
This includes legal risk but excludes strategic risk and reputational risk.

Losses from credit-related events, market-related events, and losses below a threshold of
€20,000 are excluded from the loss data used to calculate the ILM.
